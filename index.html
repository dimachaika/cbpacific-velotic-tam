01 Client & Product
Client
CB Pacific — premier industrial technology distributor & manufacturer's rep, ~30 employees, Kirkland WA. Profitable. FY starts July.
Product Being Campaigned
Velotic Scheduler (formerly GE Proficy / RobEx). Cloud-based production scheduling software. Acquired by GE from Danish company RobEx ~1.5–2 years ago. Now rebranded Velotic after GE took it private.
Go-to-Market Model
CB Pacific is the West Coast channel partner / VAR. Covers CA, OR, WA, AK (AK excluded from this campaign).
Broader Velotic Suite
Process visualization, downtime analytics, quality control, genealogy tracking. Primarily for Fortune 500 today. Scheduler is the beachhead product for mid-market.
Key Product Facts (from kickoff)
Origin
Built by Danish company RobEx, dominant in Denmark. GE purchased, now rebranded Velotic. First US market push — minimal existing US install base.
Core capability
Constraint & resource mapping (orders, machines, materials, people) → dynamic one-click re-scheduling → optional ERP/WMS/automation data tie-ins.
Key differentiator
Only cloud-based, true web/HTML interface scheduler on the market. Competitors (Siemens et al.) still desktop-based. No server required — "like Netflix, subscribe and you're done."
Pricing
Subscription only. Typically 3-year term (1- or 2-year available). ~$50K–$100K/year. Scales by number of manufacturing sites.
Case studies available
20–25 customer success stories across metals, food, discrete manufacturing, and other verticals. Gary (Velotic) to send. Critical asset — list build will be organized around cohort-to-case-study match.
Brand positioning
Lead with "Velotic, formerly GE" framing in all outreach. GE brand recognition is a significant trust lever — agreed by Mark and Gary on the call.
What the product replaces: 2–3 full-time schedulers spending 40+ hours/week managing fragile Excel spreadsheets. Post-Velotic: same output in ~5 hours/week, freeing headcount for higher-value plant tasks. Gary's line from the call: "Your business isn't spreadsheet management, and they don't want it to be."

03 Buyer Personas

This is an operations pitch, not a finance pitch — confirmed explicitly by Mark and Gary on the kickoff call. The primary buyer cares about on-time delivery, resource utilization, and making their team look like heroes.

🎯 COO / VP Operations Primary
Owns delivery commitments. Deals daily with rush orders, late schedules, customer escalations. Has signed off on scheduling headcount they'd rather reallocate. Will champion the purchase if you make them look smart.
"What if you ran the same factory with 2 fewer schedulers and 30% fewer late orders?"
🏭 Plant Manager Primary
Lives on the floor. Feels the chaos when a customer calls with a rush and the spreadsheet can't handle it. Knows the cost of every unexpected machine stop. Wants a tool, not a transformation project.
"You're spending Monday morning rebuilding last Friday's schedule — what if that took 20 minutes instead of 4 hours?"
🔗 Supply Chain Director / Director of Operations
Balancing customer orders, raw material availability, and capacity. Currently using a patchwork of Excel files, whiteboards, and tribal knowledge. Growing SKU complexity is making it worse.
"Your ERP knows what to make. It doesn't know what order to make it in, on which machine, with which team. That's the gap."
📋 Production Scheduler / Planner Caution
The person actually running the spreadsheets. Gary flagged this on the call: "We're gonna take your 40hr job and reduce it to 5hrs/week — they may not love that." Approach carefully. Frame as tool, not job replacement.
Secondary contact only. Don't lead here. Let ops leadership champion adoption; bring scheduler in during evaluation.

05 Binding Constraint TAM Analysis

The binding constraint: The company builds products to custom specification or variable customer orders — not mass-producing a single SKU at scale. They experience daily schedule changes (rush orders, delays, shifting priorities) and are still running that complexity on Excel.

Why this is non-negotiable: Large-volume producers (Coca-Cola, P&G, industrial dairies) do scheduling at the ERP level — SAP/Oracle handles it. They are not in-market. The line between fit and non-fit is custom/variable vs. standard/mass-run. Every downstream list decision flows from this.

Nuance Tier
Nuanced (relational fit)
Not every manufacturer qualifies — business model must match, not just industry label.
Constraint in Client's Own Words
"Builds to order or specification — high variability, constantly shifting priorities, juggling multiple customer orders at once. The ideal customer is someone that builds to order or to a specification for that customer." — Gary, kickoff call
Biggest Competitor
Microsoft Excel. Gary: "Often if we're the first ones to get to them to explain that there's a better way to do this, we have a really good chance of selling something."
Signal That Seals Fit
Posting for "Production Scheduler," "Supply Chain Planner," or "Operations Manager" roles at a mid-market CA/OR/WA manufacturer. That's a live signal of scheduling pain.
